DESIGN
Longitudinal cohort study using age-adjusted discrete-time 3-state multistate Markov models (interpolated Markov Chains via IMaCh v0.99r19). Data from two harmonised European longitudinal studies: SHARE (waves 1–9, 2004–2022, 18 countries) + ELSA (waves 1–10, 2002–2023, England).
COUNTRY / POPULATION
19 European countries. Nationally representative community-dwelling adults aged ≥50 years. Weighted samples per country from ~500k to >30 million.
RMD DEFINITION (verbatim)
"RMDs were identified from self-report of ever having received a doctor diagnosis of arthritis or rheumatism. We assumed that the development of arthritis was irreversible; that is, once reported, arthritis would continue to be present..."
HEALTH & WORK STATES
Healthy = no limiting long-standing illness (2 survey items). Working = currently employed or self-employed (SHARE) / paid work or self-employment in preceding month (ELSA).
COMPARISONS
Populations with RMDs vs without RMDs (overall + stratified by sex, educational attainment [ISCED-97 high vs low], physical activity [≥2 vs <2 moderate/vigorous activities/week]).
OUTCOME
Healthy Working Life Expectancy (HWLE) from age 50: average years spent both healthy and in work.
STATISTICS
Maximum likelihood estimation of transition probabilities between 3 states (healthy & working / not healthy & working / dead). HWLE weighted by age-50 state prevalence. 95% CIs reported. Stratified by country then by sex/education/PA.
PURPOSES (verbatim from Abstract)
"This study aims to estimate the extent of the reduction in healthy working life expectancy (HWLE), from age 50, for people with rheumatic and musculoskeletal diseases (RMDs) in 19 countries across Europe and highlight the need to target extending working lives through interventions and policy."
KEY FINDINGS AT A GLANCE
18/19
HWLE lower in populations with RMDs than without (except Portugal)

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS (verbatim)
"This study has identified that the amount of time that the population of people with RMDs are healthy and in work is lower than that of the population without RMDs in 18 of 19 European countries. In 6 countries, HWLE for populations with RMDs was around 50% or less..."

Critical Appraisal & Analysis

1
Value (val) — Why this study was publishable in this journal
This study addresses a highly policy-relevant question in an aging European workforce where state retirement ages are rising. It provides the first multi-country estimates of HWLE reduction attributable to RMDs using harmonised longitudinal data. The findings directly inform rheumatology practice, occupational health, and national/international policy on extending working lives. ARD is the premier rheumatology journal for high-impact epidemiological and health services research with strong clinical implications.
Verbatim (Introduction): "Across Europe, the relationship between rheumatic and musculoskeletal diseases (RMDs) and work is demanding more attention from policymakers, employers, health professionals, and people with these conditions [1–6]. ... good quality work is beneficial for individual's physical health, mental health and well-being, is an indicator of healthy ageing..."
2
What the study aimed to do (wtd) — Overarching objective
To quantify the reduction in healthy working life expectancy (HWLE) from age 50 in adults with RMDs compared to those without across 19 European countries, and to examine how this varies by sex, educational attainment, and physical activity, thereby identifying targets for interventions and policy.
Verbatim (Abstract Objectives): "This study aims to estimate the extent of the reduction in healthy working life expectancy (HWLE), from age 50, for people with rheumatic and musculoskeletal diseases (RMDs) in 19 countries across Europe and highlight the need to target extending working lives through interventions and policy."
3
Summary of previous literature (spl)
Prior work was limited to a single English study showing that adults aged 50 with osteoarthritis had approximately 4.32 fewer years of healthy working life than those without OA. No European multi-country data existed on HWLE for the broader RMD population, nor stratification by key demographic and modifiable factors.
Verbatim (Introduction): "There has been one previous study, which focused on the most common RMD in adults aged 50 and over, and estimated that adults aged 50 with osteoarthritis worked on average for 4.32 years less than those who did not have osteoarthritis in England [15]. However, there are no estimates of HWLE for people with RMDs in other countries in Europe..."
4
Critique of previous literature (cpl)
The single prior study was restricted to one country (England), one RMD subtype (osteoarthritis), and did not examine variation by sex, socioeconomic status, physical activity, or cross-country differences in policy environments. It could not inform pan-European policy or identify modifiable targets.
5
Gap between the known and unknown (gap)
No robust, comparable estimates existed of the years of healthy working life lost to RMDs across diverse European health, pension, and labour market systems. The role of modifiable factors (education, physical activity) and sex differences in HWLE within RMD populations was unknown at a multi-country level.
6
Rationale for filling the gap (rat)
Providing country-specific and stratified HWLE estimates would allow identification of high-burden populations and successful policy environments, directly informing rheumatologists, employers, and policymakers on where to target interventions to extend healthy working lives.
Verbatim (Introduction): "The overall aim is to inform health professionals in rheumatology of the extent of the reduction in healthy working life for people with RMDs and the potential need to target extending working lives through interventions and policy."
7
What they did in this study (wtdd) — Methodology
Nationally representative longitudinal data from SHARE (18 countries) and ELSA (England). Self-reported doctor-diagnosed arthritis/rheumatism (assumed irreversible). Health = absence of limiting long-standing illness; Work = employment/self-employment. 3-state multistate models (healthy&working vs other living states + absorbing death state) fitted with IMaCh software. HWLE estimated from transition probabilities, weighted by prevalence at age 50. Extensive patient and public involvement through prior interviews and dedicated workshops (selected education and physical activity as key factors).
Verbatim (Methods - Patient and public involvement): "Methods were coproduced with people with RMDs through: (1) actively listening to people’s experiences... (3) convening dedicated workshops with people with RMDs to select factors that explain differences in health and work; education and physical activity were highlighted."
8
Results consistent with previous literature (rcl)
Confirms substantial reduction in healthy working life associated with RMDs, consistent with the prior English OA study (~4 years loss). Sex gradients (generally higher HWLE in men) and strong socioeconomic (education) gradients align with known health and labour market inequalities. Physical activity benefit in most countries supports existing evidence on exercise and work capacity in RMDs.
9
Results contrary to previous literature (rtc)
Portugal showed no difference in HWLE between RMD and non-RMD populations (unique finding). In Estonia and Romania, women with RMDs had higher HWLE than men. Physical activity showed no clear benefit in 3 countries (Slovenia, Netherlands, Greece). Some countries (France) showed much smaller relative reductions than expected.
10
Recommendations for future research (rfw)
Larger datasets needed to further identify how HWLE can be increased for RMD populations. Country-specific policy analyses, intervention trials targeting physical activity and vocational retraining, and examination of interactions between sex, education, and job characteristics are warranted. The study highlights need for international, national, and local policies plus clinical approaches focusing on work outcomes.
Verbatim (How this study might affect...): "The extent that HWLE is lower in populations with RMDs in almost all countries indicates the need for policies (local, national, international), interventions and clinical approaches focusing on improving health and work for people with RMDs. ... The study indicates the need for larger datasets to further identify how HWLE can increase for populations with RMDs."
11
Points of critique — Flaws & limitations (poc)
Self-reported RMD diagnosis and health/work states introduce potential misclassification and recall bias. Assumption that arthritis is irreversible may not hold for all cases. Longitudinal studies are subject to attrition and healthy survivor bias. Country participation and number of waves varied. Physical activity measure was crude. No adjustment for comorbidities, disease severity, job physical demands, workplace accommodations, or specific RMD subtype. Wide confidence intervals in some stratified analyses (especially smaller countries).
12
Missed obvious points (mop)
The study did not differentiate between specific RMDs (e.g., RA vs OA vs others) or include disease duration/severity. No data on comorbidities, mental health, or objective clinical measures. Workplace factors (accommodations, job type, flexibility) and disability pension policies were discussed qualitatively but not modelled. Portugal’s equivalence finding was not explored in depth. Potential nocebo effects or selection of “healthy survivors” in long-running cohorts not fully addressed.
13
Relevant points worth pursuing (rpp)
Mechanistic studies explaining why some countries (e.g., Portugal, France) show smaller HWLE losses. Trials of targeted interventions (vocational rehabilitation, workplace modifications, structured physical activity programmes) in RMD populations. Qualitative research with patients and employers on barriers/facilitators. Integration of objective RMD measures and comorbidity data in future HWLE modelling. Cross-country comparative policy analysis of disability pension and return-to-work schemes.
14
Order of reasoning in the discussion (oor)
Discussion begins with key findings (significant HWLE reduction, 6 countries ≤50%, country variation). Then interprets findings in context of symptoms (pain, fatigue, disability) and system-level factors (disability pensions, workplace support). Moves to stratified results (sex, education as SES proxy, physical activity). Ends with clear implications for policy, clinical practice (work-focused assessment), and future research needs (larger datasets). Logical flow from description → explanation → actionability.
Verbatim (Discussion - Key findings): "This study has used nationally representative longitudinal data from 19 countries across Europe to estimate the average amount of time that adults who have RMDs are healthy and in work from age 50. These results indicate that RMDs are associated with a significant reduction in length of healthy working life; in 6 countries, the number of years that the population with RMDs were healthy and in work was around a half, or less, than that of the population without RMDs."
