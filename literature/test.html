ELIGIBILITY CRITERIA (verbatim)
“Patients who had been in remission or low disease activity based on the Clinical Disease Activity Index (CDAI) in the preceding phase III trial of ozoralizumab... Patients who had achieved remission and were stable or those with a low disease activity based on the Clinical Disease Activity Index (CDAI ≤10) for at least 4 weeks before enrolling in the study were eligible... stable disease activity status was assumed by confirming low disease activity or remission at 2 consecutive visits before enrolment. Patients were required to be on the same dose of methotrexate for at least 4 weeks and to have been receiving ozoralizumab 30 mg every 4 weeks at the time of randomisation.”
Detailed criteria in Supplementary Table. Concomitant glucocorticoids <10 mg/d prednisolone equivalent permitted.
INTERVENTION (3 arms)
1. Continued treatment: OZR 30 mg SC q4w + fixed MTX dose
2. OZR spacing: OZR 30 mg q6w (wk 0–24) → q8w (wk 24–48) + fixed MTX
3. MTX dose reduction: MTX reduced by 2 mg/wk every 8 weeks (possible discontinuation) + fixed OZR 30 mg q4w
Tapering mandatory unless CDAI >10. Rescue = return to previous regimen.
OUTCOME & VARIABLE

Key secondaries: CDAI remission, SDAI, DAS28-CRP/ESR, Boolean remission, HAQ-DI, ΔmTSS, flare rates, rescue success, CRP/ESR/MMP-3/RF, AEs
Exploratory: Subgroup by baseline CDAI status; functional & structural remission
STATISTICS & PURPOSES
Purpose: Assess noninferiority of OZR spacing and MTX dose reduction vs continued treatment for maintaining LDA (CDAI ≤10) at wk 48.
Analysis: PPS primary (FAS sensitivity). Newcombe method (adjusted for stratification factors) for difference & 95% CI. Noninferiority if lower CI bound > −18%. ANOVA / chi-square for other comparisons. LOCF sensitivity.
Sample size: 47/arm (141 total) for 80% power, 2.5% one-sided α, 92% assumed maintenance rate.

Critical Appraisal & Analysis

TASK 2 • Rich original text integration with section references

1. Value (val) — Why this study was publishable in this journal

This RCT directly addresses a high-priority clinical question in RA management: optimal tapering strategies for combination TNF inhibitor + methotrexate therapy after achieving treatment targets. Published in Annals of the Rheumatic Diseases (IF 24.0, top-tier rheumatology journal), it provides the first head-to-head evidence using ozoralizumab (a novel NANOBODY TNF inhibitor) and includes a unique three-arm design comparing continued treatment, TNF spacing, and MTX dose reduction. The findings have immediate implications for EULAR recommendations on tapering and real-world practice, especially given the emphasis on minimising long-term drug exposure while maintaining remission.
Verbatim Excerpts:
“This randomised controlled trial provides new evidence on the efficacy and safety of medication tapering with a TNF inhibitor and methotrexate in patients who have achieved remission or low disease activity by directly comparing continued treatment, ozoralizumab spacing, and methotrexate dose reduction strategies.” (WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS)

“In patients who have achieved remission with TNF inhibitor plus methotrexate therapy, extending the TNF inhibitor dosing interval or reducing the methotrexate dose is feasible and favourable under careful clinical monitoring.” (HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY)

2. What the study aimed to do (wtd) — Overarching objective

The primary objective was to demonstrate noninferiority of two tapering strategies (ozoralizumab interval spacing or stepwise methotrexate dose reduction) compared with continued full-dose combination therapy in maintaining low disease activity (CDAI ≤10) at 48 weeks in patients with RA who had achieved remission or LDA on ozoralizumab + MTX.
Verbatim Excerpts:
“This study aimed to assess the noninferiority of spacing a tumour necrosis factor (TNF) inhibitor or reducing the methotrexate dose with continued treatment in patients with rheumatoid arthritis.” (ABSTRACT — Objectives)

“The primary endpoint was a noninferiority of low disease activity maintenance at week 48 with a prespecified noninferiority margin of −18%.” (ABSTRACT — Methods)

3. Summary of previous literature (spl)

Prior studies (RRR, HONOR, PRESERVE, STRASS, DRESS, MIRACLE, MUSICA, ARCTIC REWIND) have explored TNF inhibitor dose reduction/discontinuation or MTX tapering in RA, generally showing that deep remission predicts successful tapering. However, standardised protocols for simultaneous tapering of both TNF inhibitor and MTX in combination therapy remained unestablished, particularly with newer agents such as ozoralizumab.
Verbatim Excerpts:
“Although several studies have been conducted on such tapering strategies [4−12], a standardised approach for combining methotrexate and a tumour necrosis factor (TNF) inhibitor is yet to be established.” (INTRODUCTION)

“As the RRR and HONOR trials revealed, deep remission achievement is the key to successful discontinuation of TNF inhibitors [4,9].” (DISCUSSION)

4. Critique of previous literature (cpl)

Many previous trials were limited by heterogeneous populations, lack of direct three-arm comparisons, focus on single-drug tapering, or short follow-up. Some (e.g. PRESERVE with etanercept) showed success with dose reduction, while others (STRASS, ARCTIC REWIND) demonstrated increased flare risk. Differences in MTX dosing, remission depth definition, and drug pharmacokinetics complicate generalisability.
Verbatim Excerpts:
“Although the overall study design was different, the PRESERVE trial demonstrated that reducing etanercept from 50 to 25 mg/wk... showed comparable rates... This difference may be ascribed to the different kinds of TNF inhibitors... or strategies.” (DISCUSSION)

“The STRASS trial... demonstrated that extending the etanercept or adalimumab dosing interval was not equivalent to continuing the treatment [28].” (DISCUSSION)

5. Gap between the known and unknown (gap)

There was a clear evidence gap regarding the feasibility of tapering both components of combination therapy (TNF inhibitor + MTX) in a single RCT, especially with a next-generation TNF inhibitor (ozoralizumab) that has unique pharmacokinetics (small size, long half-life ~18 days, no Fc domain). The optimal sequence, depth of remission required, and patient selection criteria for successful de-escalation were not well defined.
Verbatim Excerpts:
“Ozoralizumab is a uniquely structured anti-TNFα biological agent... Its small size and lack of an Fc domain allows rapid efficacy... We used this TNF inhibitor to establish evidence for the optimal tapering strategy for TNF inhibitors and methotrexate...” (INTRODUCTION)

6. Rationale for filling the gap (rat)

The SORAIRO trial was designed to fill this gap by leveraging a well-characterised cohort from the ozoralizumab phase III program (stable on combination therapy for median 3.9 years) and testing practical, protocolised tapering regimens. The study directly informs whether spacing the unique ozoralizumab molecule or reducing MTX is safe in patients who have already achieved remission/LDA, aligning with EULAR recommendations to taper after sustained remission.
Verbatim Excerpts:
“Therapeutic strategies after achieving treatment goals have long been discussed. Recent international guidelines recommend not discontinuing antirheumatic drugs but tapering them to minimise adverse events (AEs) and reduce healthcare costs while maintaining a remission status [2,3].” (INTRODUCTION)

7. What they did in this study (wtdd) — Methodology

149 patients from the HOSHIZORA long-term extension were randomised 1:1:1. PPS = 144. Primary analysis used Newcombe-adjusted CIs for noninferiority. Open-label design with rescue protocol (reversion to prior dosing) for flares (CDAI >10). Rigorous outcome assessment included CDAI, multiple composite indices, HAQ-DI, and radiographic scoring (mTSS) by two blinded readers.
Verbatim Excerpts:
“The SORAIRO trial... is a multicentre, open-label, randomised, parallel-group noninferiority study... Patients were randomised... in a 1:1:1 ratio to the continued treatment, ozoralizumab spacing, or methotrexate dose reduction groups.” (METHODS — Study design)

“In the ozoralizumab spacing group, ozoralizumab 30 mg was administered every 6 weeks for the first 24 weeks and every 8 weeks for the subsequent 24 weeks...” (METHODS)

8. Results consistent with previous literature (rcl)

In the baseline remission subgroup, remission maintenance rates were nearly identical (~77–79%) across all three arms — consistent with RRR, HONOR and DREAM registry findings that deep remission predicts successful tapering. Rescue therapy restored LDA in >83% of patients, aligning with previous reports of 80–100% recovery after re-initiation of therapy.
Verbatim Excerpts:
“In the subgroup of patients with baseline remission (CDAI ≤2.8...), the maintenance rates of remission at week 48 in this baseline remission subgroup were comparable: 77.4%, 76.7%, and 79.2%, respectively.” (RESULTS)

“Many previous studies have shown that 80% to 100% of patients who experienced disease flares following medication tapering regained low disease activity...” (DISCUSSION)

9. Results contrary to previous literature (rtc)

Overall (including LDA patients), neither tapering strategy met noninferiority — contrasting with PRESERVE (etanercept dose reduction) and DRESS (TNF spacing). This may reflect lower baseline MTX doses in this Japanese cohort, the unique properties of ozoralizumab, or stricter CDAI-based flare definition. The higher flare rate in the overall population highlights that LDA (vs deep remission) is insufficient for safe tapering.
Verbatim Excerpts:
“Our study showed that spacing TNF inhibitor administration was not noninferior to continued treatment in patients with low disease activity.” (DISCUSSION)

“The methotrexate dose reduction was not noninferior to the continued treatment...” (DISCUSSION)

10. Recommendations for future research (rfw)

The authors highlight the need for longer follow-up to capture late flares, integrated analyses combining prior and current trial data for exact remission duration, biomarker-guided personalised tapering, and studies in more diverse ethnic populations. They also call for optimised tapering protocols and cost-effectiveness analyses.
Verbatim Excerpts:
“a longer observation period is necessary to confirm the long-term stability and safety of these de-escalation strategies.” (DISCUSSION — Limitations)

“Further investigations are required to determine the optimal strategy for tapering TNF inhibitors or methotrexate.” (DISCUSSION)

11. Points of critique (flaws & limitations) (poc)

Open-label design introduces potential nocebo bias and assessment bias. Sample size, although reaching target, remains modest. Ozoralizumab’s unique structure (NANOBODY, t½ ~18 days, no Fc) may limit extrapolation to monoclonal antibodies or fusion proteins. Remission/LDA confirmation was only 4 weeks × 2 visits (short per EULAR/ACR guidance of ≥6 months). 48-week follow-up may miss late flares. MTX tapering schedule introduced heterogeneity.
Verbatim Excerpts (from Discussion Limitations):
“First, this was an open-label trial... Second, the sample size was relatively small... Third, ozoralizumab is a TNF inhibitor with unique structures... Fourth, the 48-week follow-up period after reaching the final dose may not have been adequate for capturing late-onset flares... Fifth, to our regret, the exact duration of remission or low disease activity in our trial could not be evaluated...”

12. Missed obvious points (mop)

The trial did not measure ozoralizumab trough concentrations or anti-drug antibodies at flare timepoints to correlate pharmacokinetics with loss of response. No health economic or quality-of-life beyond HAQ-DI analysis was presented. Patient preference and shared decision-making around tapering were not formally assessed. The study population represents “healthy survivors” with long-term stability on the specific combination.
Analysis note: While the authors acknowledge the “healthy survivor” bias and call for integrated analyses, direct PK/PD correlation at the time of flare would have strengthened mechanistic insight into why some patients flared early after spacing.

13. Relevant points worth pursuing (rpp)

Future work should explore biomarker-driven (e.g. MMP-3, RF, imaging) or remission-duration-guided tapering algorithms. Head-to-head comparisons of ozoralizumab spacing vs other TNFis, implementation studies in routine care, and longer-term (>2 year) outcomes of successful tapering are high priority. The high rescue success rate supports reversible tapering as a safe clinical strategy.
Verbatim Excerpts:
“These consistent findings provide reassurance to rheumatologists and patients when considering tapering medications in clinical practice.” (DISCUSSION)

14. Order of reasoning in the discussion (oor)

The Discussion follows a logical clinical reasoning flow: (1) Summary of main result (tapering not NI overall but comparable in remission subgroup); (2) Comparison with key prior trials (PRESERVE, STRASS, DRESS, RRR, HONOR) explaining discrepancies; (3) Interpretation of MTX tapering success rate and variability; (4) Safety profile; (5) Detailed limitations (open-label, sample size, drug uniqueness, follow-up duration, remission duration unknown); (6) Clinical implications and final conclusion that tapering is reasonable in remission under monitoring.
Structure summary: Starts with key positive finding in remission patients → contrasts with overall LDA population → mechanistic/clinical explanation → safety → limitations → practical recommendation. This mirrors high-quality EULAR-style discussion style.
