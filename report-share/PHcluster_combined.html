Group 1 · PAH

Rare: prevalence 15–60 / million, incidence 5–10 / million / yr (Europe) [3]. Mean RHC-confirmed prevalence ~3.7 / 100 000 [4]. GBD 2021: ~192 000 prevalent cases globally (age-std. 2.28 / 100 000) [5].

Group 2 · Left heart disease

Most common form. Ontario cohort (n=50 529): Group 2 in 68.5% of adult PH (alone 34.2%; with Group 3 29.3%). PH complicates only ~3.6% of people with left heart disease [6].

Group 3 · Lung disease / hypoxia

Second most frequent. Group 3 present in ~47% of adult PH. PH is uncommon among all lung-disease patients (~0.7%), yet with Group 2 accounts for nearly all population-level PH [2,6].

Group 4 · CTEPH  ·  Group 5 · Multifactorial

G4: ~3–30 / million; ~0.5–3% after PE; ~9% of adult PH [6–8]. G5: unclear / multifactorial (hematologic, systemic, metabolic) — least well quantified population estimates.

Phenotypes in Pulmonary Hypertension

Weatherald J, Hemnes AR, Maron BA, Mielniczuk LM, Gerges C, Price LC, Hoeper MM, Humbert M
Eur Respir J 2024; 64: 2301633 · DOI: 10.1183/13993003.01633-2023

Why Phenotype Pulmonary Hypertension?

A clinically heterogeneous disease

PH is defined as a resting mean PAP > 20 mmHg. Genetics, pathobiology, clinical features and outcomes all vary widely.

  • Phenotype — a cluster of visible properties (demographic, physiological, imaging, molecular).
  • Endotype — a shared genetic/molecular mechanism, opening the door to precision medicine.

A clearer phenotype map sharpens diagnosis, prognostication and treatment selection.

Five clinical groups

1  Pulmonary arterial hypertension 2  Left heart disease 3  Lung disease / hypoxia 4  Chronic thromboembolic (CTEPD) 5  Unclear / multifactorial

A framework evolving over 50 years (WHO 1973 → 2022 ESC/ERS).

Methodology: Deep Phenotyping & Omics

Phenotyping starts at the bedside — history, imaging, lung function and right heart catheterisation — then layers on advanced computational discovery.

  • Multi-omics: genome, transcriptome, proteome, metabolome (and exhaled "volatolomics").
  • Computational tools: machine learning, LASSO, Shapley values, unsupervised clustering, network medicine.
  • Longitudinal data: EMRs, wearables, implantable haemodynamic sensors.

Goal — distil "big data" into parsimonious, treatable traits.

Three Clinical Phenotypes Within PAH

Beyond the classical PAH phenotype (younger, female), two phenotypes emerge in older patients:

  • Left heart phenotype — ≥65 yrs, HFpEF risk factors, frequent atrial fibrillation.
  • Lung phenotype — ≥65 yrs, male-predominant, heavy smoking history, very low DLCO.

Sex and atrial-fibrillation burden cleanly separate the three.

PAH With a Left Heart Phenotype

Features signalling latent HFpEF despite a pre-capillary RHC reading (PAWP ≤ 15 mmHg at rest).

  • Age ≥ 65, obesity, diabetes, coronary disease, hypertension.
  • 1 in 3 to 1 in 2 have atrial fibrillation (vs <10% in classical PAH).
  • Exercise / fluid loading can unmask PAWP ≥ 25 mmHg.

Recommended treatment: oral monotherapy, then individualised escalation.

Genetic Sub-Phenotypes in PAH

BMPR2 — more severe haemodynamics, higher mortality; ground-glass "halos" on CT.

EIF2AK4 — pulmonary veno-occlusive disease; low DLCO, hypoxaemia, poor therapy response.

TBX4 — small patella syndrome, peribronchial cysts, low DLCO.

SOX17 — haemoptysis, tortuous vessels, neovascularisation.

KDR · ACVRL1/ENG/GDF2 — parenchymal change; HHT-modulated phenotypes.

Each genotype carries a distinctive radiological and prognostic signature — Refs [64–76]

Group 2: Reversible vs Persistent PH in LHD

Reversible PH

Minimal vessel change · adaptive RV remodelling · resolves with treatment of the underlying valve/heart disease (GDMT, TAVR, mitral repair, septal ablation).

Persistent (Cpc-)PH

Progressive arterial & venous remodelling · maladaptive RV · elevated PVR (>2 WU) and reduced compliance that persist despite diuresis.

Combined pre-/post-capillary PH should be viewed as an independent pathology, not a secondary phenomenon — Refs [50, 81–85]

Group 3: The COPD Pulmonary Vascular Phenotype

Most COPD-PH is mild (mPAP 20–35 mmHg). But a distinct cluster shows severe PH with only mild airflow obstruction.

  • Lower DLCO, resting hypoxaemia, circulatory (not ventilatory) exercise limit.
  • Severe PH = PVR > 5 WU → 1-yr mortality of 24–30%.

Considerable overlap with "PAH + lung phenotype".

Group 3: PH in Interstitial Lung Disease

Even mild PH worsens survival in ILD. Severity is graded haemodynamically by PVR.

  • PVR > 5 WU defines severe PH (2022 ESC/ERS).
  • PVR > 8 WU is the best discriminator of worse prognosis.
  • Inhaled treprostinil (INCREASE) improved exercise capacity & delayed progression.

Group 5: Sarcoidosis-Associated PH (SAPH)

The best-characterised Group 5 entity. Careful multimodal work-up guides management.

  • Most common with lung fibrosis (Scadding stage 4), but can occur without.
  • Guidelines suggest monotherapy when FVC < 50% predicted.

Distinct sub-phenotypes

Compressive — granulomatous/fibrosing mediastinal compression of vessels → PET-CT-guided immunomodulation ± stenting.

Veno-occlusive — poor therapy response; prompt transplant referral.

Refs: Nunes 2006 [146]; WASOG [147]; Samaranayake 2021 [148]

Key Findings

① Comorbidities reshape PAH — left-heart & lung phenotypes resemble Group 2 / 3 and respond less well to combination therapy.

② Severe pulmonary vasculopathy can occur with only mild airway or parenchymal disease — a recurring "vascular phenotype".

③ Omics & ML reveal distinct immune, autoimmune and genetic sub-phenotypes with prognostic value.

④ PAWP at a single time-point is fragile — Cpc-PH and HFpEF-PH overlap heavily with PAH's left-heart phenotype.

Validating phenotypic / endotypic signatures as treatable traits remains the central unmet need.

Conclusion

The current five-group classification is a durable framework — but considerable heterogeneity remains within each group.

Integrating clinical data, imaging, multi-omics and longitudinal exposures promises sharper sub-phenotypes and endotypes.

The next iterations of the classification should enable precision therapy, enriched trials and better outcomes.
